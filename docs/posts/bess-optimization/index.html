Project Overview

In modern electricity grids, the high penetration of intermittent renewable energy resources (such as wind and solar) creates significant price volatility. Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) play a critical role in stabilizing the grid and capturing value through arbitrage—charging during periods of low (or negative) electricity prices and discharging during periods of high demand.

This project implements an end-to-end algorithmic pipeline to maximize the profit of a 10 MW / 20 MWh battery operating in a day-ahead wholesale electricity market. The pipeline consists of two main stages: 1. Day-Ahead Price Forecasting: An XGBoost regressor models wholesale electricity prices using historical price lags and rolling volatility features, tuned using Optuna. 2. Optimal Dispatch Scheduling: A Mixed-Integer Linear Program (MILP) consumes the price forecasts to solve for the profit-maximizing physical dispatch schedule, subject to battery constraints (efficiency, state-of-charge limits, charging/discharging bounds).

Methodology

1. Wholesale Electricity Price Forecasting

To make profitable charging and discharging decisions, the system must forecast hourly prices for the upcoming day. * Model: XGBoost Regressor. * Features: Time-delay embeddings (lags), rolling average volatility, hour-of-day indicators, and historical demand trends. * Validation: Time-Series Cross Validation (to prevent data leakage). * Optimization: Hyperparameters tuned via Optuna.

2. Battery Dispatch Optimization

Once the prices \(\hat{p}_t\) are forecast for each hour \(t \in \{1, \dots, 24\}\), they are fed into a Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) model to determine the optimal action \(u_t\) (charge, discharge, or idle).

Objective Function

Maximize the day’s net revenue:

\[\max \sum_{t=1}^{24} \left( p^{\text{discharge}}_t \cdot \eta_{\text{discharge}} \cdot x^{\text{discharge}}_t - p^{\text{charge}}_t \cdot \frac{1}{\eta_{\text{charge}}} \cdot x^{\text{charge}}_t \right)\]

where: * \(x^{\text{discharge}}_t \ge 0\) and \(x^{\text{charge}}_t \ge 0\) are the power discharged or charged at hour \(t\) (in MW). * \(\eta_{\text{discharge}}\) and \(\eta_{\text{charge}}\) are the discharging and charging efficiencies (e.g., 90%). * \(p^{\text{discharge}}_t, p^{\text{charge}}_t\) are market prices.

Physical Constraints

  • State of Charge (SoC) Dynamics: \(SoC_t = SoC_{t-1} + x^{\text{charge}}_t \cdot \eta_{\text{charge}} - \frac{x^{\text{discharge}}_t}{\eta_{\text{discharge}}}\).
  • SoC Bounds: \(SoC_{\min} \le SoC_t \le SoC_{\max}\) (e.g., \(10\% \le SoC_t \le 90\%\)).
  • Power Limits: \(0 \le x^{\text{discharge}}_t \le P_{\max}\) and \(0 \le x^{\text{charge}}_t \le P_{\max}\) (10 MW limit).
  • Mutual Exclusivity: Binary decision variables ensure the battery does not charge and discharge simultaneously.

Code & Artifacts

All python code, notebooks, and mathematical scripts are located in the project folder:

  • Jupyter Notebook: bess_final.ipynb (End-to-end walk-through of the data cleaning, modeling, optimization, and backtesting).
  • Core Optimization Script: bess.py (Pure Python implementation of the MILP model using pulp).
  • Quantitative Backtesting: quant_bess.py (Simulates revenue generation over a historical test period, comparing forecasted optimization profits against an upper-bound “perfect foresight” oracle).
  • Financial Models: finance_project.py (Additional cash flow projections and NPV calculations for BESS assets).
